Fuck SOLID

Sidebar: SOLID is a well-known set of object-oriented design principles. In this article, we'll explore why you might choose to step away from this commonly accepted wisdom.

In the realm of object-oriented programming, SOLID principles have long been upheld as the holy grail of design. However, like all mantras, they're not without their detractors. Some developers even go as far as to say, "Forget SOLID." But why? Let's delve into the reasons.

First, let's refresh our memory on what SOLID stands for:

  • Single Responsibility Principle (SRP)
  • Open/Closed Principle (OCP)
  • Liskov Substitution Principle (LSP)
  • Interface Segregation Principle (ISP)
  • Dependency Inversion Principle (DIP)

These principles promote decoupling, code reuse, and easier maintenance among other benefits. But let's not forget - principles are guidelines, not rules. They're not one-size-fits-all, and strict adherence can sometimes be counterproductive. Here are some reasons you might choose to abandon SOLID:

Overengineering #

SOLID principles can lead to an explosion of classes and interfaces, as each class has only one responsibility and uses dependency inversion to stay decoupled. However, there's a point of diminishing returns where the complexity introduced by this level of modularity begins to outweigh its benefits.

Unnecessary Abstraction #

The Open/Closed Principle and the Liskov Substitution Principle encourage the use of inheritance and polymorphism. While these can be powerful tools, they also introduce additional layers of abstraction, which can make code harder to understand and maintain.

Context Matters #

What works in one context may not work in another. While SOLID principles are generally good practices, they might not be the best fit for every project or team. For small projects or prototypes, strict adherence to SOLID can overcomplicate things.

So, what's the alternative?

Programming is about trade-offs. While SOLID principles provide one framework for making these trade-offs, they are not the only one. Other methodologies, like KISS (Keep it Simple, Stupid), YAGNI (You Aren't Gonna Need It), and DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself), offer different perspectives on how to balance code complexity, readability, and maintainability.

Additionally, functional programming paradigms are gaining popularity and offer an entirely different perspective on how software should be designed. Principles like immutability and pure functions could be a refreshing change if you're feeling stifled by the rigidity of SOLID.

In conclusion, SOLID principles are a valuable tool in a programmer's toolbox, but they're not a silver bullet. Don't be afraid to think outside the box and consider alternative methodologies that might be a better fit for your specific situation. Happy coding!
